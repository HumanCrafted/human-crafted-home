## What This Is

A reference for writing new content on humancrafted.co that sounds like Jon wrote it, not an AI. Built by studying the existing site content: blog posts, the More page intro, About Me, Process, project descriptions, and doc pages.

---

## Who Jon Is (for context)

Mechanical engineer by training (MSOE, class of 2000). Over 25 years in product design spanning agencies, a corporate OEM (Johnson Health Tech), and then founding Human Crafted in 2016 as a solo studio and design brand. Based in Madison, Wisconsin. Father. Maker at heart. Also into espresso, synth music, and tools of all kinds.

The throughline: Jon solves problems by building things quickly, learning from the result, and iterating. He calls himself a "Creative Engineer" and treats that range as a superpower, not a compromise.

## Voice and Tone

### The short version

Write like a smart friend explaining something they care about over coffee. Direct, warm, occasionally playful, never performative. The confidence comes from experience, not posturing.

### Tone qualities to hit

**Conversational but grounded.** Jon writes the way he'd talk to a peer. Contractions are natural. Sentences vary in length. Some are blunt and short. Others unspool a thought across a longer line. He doesn't over-explain, but he also doesn't assume you know his world.

**Confident without being self-important.** There's a quiet assurance that comes from having actually done the work. He'll say "I believe" or "we believe" without hedging, but he won't lecture. He's more likely to share what he's learned than tell you what to think.

**Honest and a little self-aware.** He'll admit when something was hard ("Setting sail as an entrepreneur and freelance designer wasn't an easy decision") or when something didn't work out. He doesn't sugarcoat, but he doesn't dwell either. He moves forward.

**Energetic when the moment calls for it.** Not hype-man energy. More like genuine momentum. He uses bold text for emphasis sparingly, and when he does, it lands. He'll end a piece with "Here we go!" and it doesn't feel forced because he earned it.

**Practical over philosophical.** He has a design philosophy, but he always grounds it in what you actually do. Even abstract ideas ("what a person values is complicated") quickly connect back to a concrete action ("make them something that helps, so we can quickly learn if it adds value").

### Tone qualities to avoid

- Corporate polish or marketing speak ("leveraging synergies," "holistic solutions")
- Overly academic or jargon-heavy writing (no "paradigm shifts" or "methodological frameworks")
- False humility or excessive self-deprecation
- Breathless enthusiasm or forced excitement
- Stiff formality ("It is with great pleasure that...")
- Generic inspirational quotes or platitudes
- Robotic sentence structures where every sentence follows the same pattern

## Sentence-Level Patterns

### Sentence structure

Jon writes in a rhythm that alternates between direct, punchy statements and more flowing thoughts. He'll often follow a longer, complex sentence with a short one that snaps the point into focus.

Example of this rhythm: "Product design is serious business, but sometimes maybe a bit too serious. Complicated by layers of management, silos, titles, degrees, egos, roadmaps, and SOPs. More often than not, no matter how large or small your company is, it's best to just let loose and make something."

Notice the fragment ("Complicated by layers of..."). Jon uses fragments comfortably. They add pace.

### Word choice

Simple, direct words over fancy ones. "Make" over "fabricate." "Try" over "attempt." "Fast" over "expeditious." But he's not afraid of specificity when it matters: he'll name exact tools, materials, dimensions, and processes.

He uses "we" when talking about Human Crafted as a brand or philosophy, and "I" when it's personal experience or opinion. The shift is natural and context-driven.

### Emphasis

Bold text is used for key ideas, not decoration. Usually a short phrase that carries the core message of a paragraph. Examples from the existing writing:

- "**Newsflash... there is no perfect.**"
- "**To us, better means it creates more value.**"
- "**to learn**"
- "**MAKING PROGRESS.**"

The all-caps "MAKING PROGRESS" is rare. Most emphasis is bold. Italics appear occasionally for internal thoughts or hypothetical questions.

### Punctuation

- Em dashes show up occasionally, but not constantly
- Ellipsis (...) appears in casual spots, especially in taglines and transitions: "and above all... making progress"
- He uses blockquote formatting for imagined dialogue or rhetorical back-and-forth
- One emoji per page maximum, and only when it genuinely adds personality (the superhero emoji on the More page is a good example)

## Content Structure Patterns

### Blog posts / long-form writing

Jon's blog posts follow a loose pattern: open with a clear, simple statement of the idea, then develop it through a mix of personal story, philosophy, and practical application. He uses H3 subheadings to break longer pieces into digestible sections, and those headings tend to be short and punchy ("Made." / "Better.").

He likes the rhetorical device of an imagined skeptic. In "Made Better," he uses blockquotes as a back-and-forth dialogue with "-them" and "-us." This keeps things dynamic and anticipates reader objections without being defensive.

Posts tend to end on a forward-looking, energetic note rather than a tidy summary. He doesn't wrap things up with a bow. He pushes forward.

### The More page / bio-style writing

The More page intro is the clearest example of Jon's personal voice. Key things to note:

- Opens with "Hi, I'm Jon" — immediate, friendly, no preamble
- Weaves professional history into a personal narrative rather than listing accomplishments
- Links naturally to other parts of the site (expertise, past work, writing) without feeling like a sitemap
- Ends with a clear invitation to explore further or get in touch

### Doc pages (tools, process, etc.)

These are more utilitarian but still have personality. They tend to:

- Open with a one-line summary or specs in bold
- Use H3 headings for subsections
- Include lists where they're genuinely useful (capabilities, materials, process steps)
- Reference specific projects where applicable
- End with "More details coming soon!" if the page is still growing (this is honest and sets expectations)

### Project pages

Most project pages are minimal right now (front matter plus a short headline or "More story to come"). When they do have content, they tend toward brief, factual descriptions. The front matter itself tells most of the story: title, categories, tools used, version number.

When writing project descriptions, keep them short and focused on what the thing does and why it exists, not a blow-by-blow of how it was made (unless the process is the interesting part).

## Vocabulary and Phrases Jon Uses

### Words and phrases that feel like Jon

- "make something" / "make it" / "making progress"
- "let loose"
- "fine tune" / "fine tuned"
- "rapid" / "rapidly" (as in rapid prototyping, rapid experimentation)
- "build to learn"
- "cut through the chaos"
- "bring ideas to life"
- "made better" / "make our lives better"
- "the next discrete and logical step"
- "a bit better" / "a bit smoother"
- "experiment" (he treats products as experiments)
- "value" / "customer value" / "adds value"
- "reduce risk"
- "can-do, maker spirit"
- "super power" / "super-wide range"
- "path less traveled"
- "version 1.0" (as a mindset, not just software)

### Words and phrases to avoid

- "leverage" / "utilize" / "optimize" (prefer "use," "make the most of")
- "innovative" / "disruptive" (too buzzy for this voice)
- "passionate about" (overused; Jon shows passion through specificity)
- "deep dive" / "unpack" (consultant-speak)
- "at the end of the day"
- "it goes without saying"
- "in today's fast-paced world"
- Any sentence that starts with "As a..."

## Formatting Conventions for the Site

### Front matter template (docs)

```yaml
---
layout: doc
title: Page Title
slug: page-title
main_image:
featured: false
tags:
  - relevant-tag
published_date: YYYY-MM-DD
gallery_images:
version: "1.0"
draft: false
---
```

### Front matter template (projects)

```yaml
---
layout: project
title: Project Name
slug: project-name
main_image: "![[project-name-thumbnail.svg]]"
featured: false
categories:
  - category-name
published_date: YYYY-MM-DD
draft: false
gallery_images:
headline: Short tagline
version: "1.0"
tools:
process:
---
```

### Linking conventions

- Use Obsidian wiki-links where possible: `[[page-name|Display Text]]`
- For images: `![[image-filename.ext]]`
- For links to docs from other docs: `[Display Text](_docs/filename.md)`
- For linking to the homepage: `[text](../index.md)` or `[text](/index.md)`
- The Obsidian plugin handles conversion to Jekyll URLs at build time

### Heading levels

- H1 is handled by the layout (from front matter title) — never put an H1 in content
- H2 for major sections
- H3 for subsections (most common in doc pages)
- Keep heading text short and direct

### General formatting

- Bold for key phrases and emphasis, not for entire sentences
- Italics sparingly, mainly for internal questions or hypotheticals
- One emoji max per page, only when it genuinely fits
- Lists when they add clarity, prose when they don't
- "More details coming soon!" is an acceptable placeholder for pages in progress
- Keep paragraphs short. Three to five sentences is a good range for the web.

## Writing Checklist

Before publishing new content, ask:

1. Does this sound like it was written by a person who makes things for a living?
2. Is the opening line direct and interesting, not throat-clearing?
3. Are there any sentences that sound like they came from a press release?
4. Is bold text used only for genuinely important phrases?
5. Would Jon actually say this out loud to someone?
6. Does it connect back to making, building, or learning somewhere?
7. Are the headings short and clear?
8. Is there a natural link to another part of the site where it makes sense?
